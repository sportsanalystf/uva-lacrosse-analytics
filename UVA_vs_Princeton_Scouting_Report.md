# Opponent scouting report: #18 Princeton at Virginia, March 11

**Princeton enters Charlottesville at 2-2 with elite senior talent but a glaring draw-control vulnerability that Virginia is built to exploit.** The Tigers lost their all-time leading scorer McKenzie Blake and all-time draw specialist Sophie Whiteway to graduation, and the draw-circle deficit has been the defining storyline of their early season — losing draws **6-20** to Loyola and **8-21** to Maryland. Virginia's Kate Galica, averaging **7.4 draw controls per game** (22nd nationally), represents Princeton's worst matchup nightmare. The Tigers remain dangerous, however: they feature four preseason All-Americans, a senior goalkeeper averaging **11.25 saves per game**, and an attack duo in Jami MacDonald and Haven Dora that ranks among the nation's most productive. Virginia, now **4-4 (3-1 ACC)** after consecutive road wins at Pitt and Florida State, has momentum and owns the head-to-head series **24-19**, including wins in the last three meetings.

## The attack trio of Morrisroe, MacDonald, and Dora drives everything

Princeton's offense runs through three distinct weapons, each requiring a specific defensive response.

**#3 Meg Morrisroe — Jr., Attack, 5'8", Huntington, N.Y.**
The team's leading goal scorer has been on fire. Through 4 games: approximately **16 goals, 3+ assists, 19+ points**. She shot **.632** through her first three games (12 goals on 19 shots) and ranked **8th nationally in goals per game (4.0)**. Against Loyola, she scored all 5 goals in the second half, showing an ability to take over games. She is Princeton's most dangerous finisher — fast release, comfortable from multiple angles, and increasingly confident in her junior year after notching 38 goals as a sophomore. **Virginia must account for Morrisroe as the primary scoring threat.**

**#17 Jami MacDonald (C) — Sr., Attack, 5'7", Georgetown, Ontario**
MacDonald reached her **200th career point** at Harvard with an 8-point masterpiece (4G/4A). Her 2025 season: **58G, 34A, 92 points** (2nd-Team All-Ivy). She is a **USA Lacrosse 2nd-Team Preseason All-American** and past Tewaaraton Watch List selection who won silver with Team Canada U-20. Through 4 games in 2026: approximately **9-11 goals, 8+ assists, 17-19 points**. MacDonald is the most complete player on the roster — she creates for herself and others, is deadly in settled situations, and elevates her game in big moments. She had 3G/2A in the 2025 meeting vs. Virginia that Princeton lost 11-13.

**#7 Haven Dora — Sr., Attack, 5'3", Spring Lake, N.J.**
Princeton's **all-time career assists leader** (107 entering 2026, now 118+) is the engine of the offense. Her 2025 season: **29G, 60A, 89 points** (1st-Team All-Ivy, program single-season assist record). She is the **only Ivy Leaguer named USA Lacrosse 1st-Team Preseason All-American** and a past Tewaaraton Watch List selection and USA U-20 gold medalist. Through 4 games in 2026: approximately **4G, 11+ assists, 15+ points**. Dora is primarily a facilitator — her **6-assist game at Harvard** was one shy of the program record. She also posted a career-high **7 draw controls** at Harvard, suggesting she is taking on draw specialist duties. **Disrupting Dora's passing lanes is the single most impactful thing Virginia's defense can do.**

**Key assist connections to watch:** Dora feeds MacDonald and Morrisroe most frequently. Against Harvard, Dora had 6 assists while MacDonald had 4 assists, creating a web of connectivity that made Princeton's offense nearly impossible to contain. The Dora-to-MacDonald and Dora-to-Morrisroe connections are the primary passing lanes. MacDonald also creates for Morrisroe and vice versa. When these three players combine for 5+ assists in a game, Princeton wins.

## Amelia Hughes is a battle-tested wall in goal

**#12 Amelia Hughes — Sr., GK, 5'6", Wilton, Conn.**

Hughes has started **all 58 of Princeton's games** over her four-year career — every single one. She has recorded **double-digit saves in all 4 games this season**: 12 vs. Loyola, 12 vs. Rutgers, 11 vs. Maryland, 10 vs. Harvard. Her 2026 totals: **45 saves, 11.25 saves per game**.

**Career profile:**
- **2025 (career bests):** .481 save percentage, 11.79 GAA, 180 saves (4th nationally), 15 wins
- **Career saves entering 2026:** 509 (7th all-time at Princeton), now approximately **554**
- **2024 Ivy League Goalkeeper of the Year**, 3x All-Ivy selection
- USA Lacrosse 2026 Preseason All-American Honorable Mention
- Made a season-high 16 saves in the 2025 NCAA Quarterfinal loss to #1 North Carolina

**Scouting Hughes:** She sees a high volume of shots and thrives on activity — Princeton's defensive scheme funnels shots to areas where Hughes is comfortable. Her **.481 save percentage** in 2025 is strong but not elite by national standards; she compensates with volume and consistency. She is better at stopping point-blank shots from the 8-meter than long-range attempts, based on the shot profiles in her game logs. **The key to beating Hughes is shot selection and placement** — avoid feeding her rhythm with predictable shots. Virginia should emphasize quick ball movement to create unsettled looks and attack from the wings rather than top-center.

**Backup:** Elizabeth Gonnella (So.) appeared in 8 games as a freshman (.465 SV%, 11.29 GAA). Freshman Jackie Feldman was an Inside Lacrosse Power 100 recruit and 2x HS All-American.

## Draw controls are Princeton's Achilles' heel

The graduation of **Sophie Whiteway** — Princeton's all-time draw control leader with **249 career draws** — has left a void that may be the defining factor on March 11.

| vs Rutgers | **8** | 16 | W, 15-9 |

**Princeton has been demolished on the draw circle in 3 of 4 games.** They lost the draw control battle 6-20, 8-16, and 8-21 in their first three games — winning only when their defense and finishing efficiency overcame the possession deficit (the Rutgers win). At Harvard, there were signs of improvement with Dora posting 7 draw controls, but the sample is small.

**Who takes draws for Princeton now?** The role is being split among Haven Dora, Colette Quinn, Ella Sloan, Maggie Bacigalupo, and possibly freshman Becca Kirschner (105 HS draws). Defender Abigail Roberts contributed 78 draw controls in 2025, so she factors in as well. But none of these players replicate Whiteway's dominance.

**The correlation is stark:** When Princeton loses draws badly, they lose games. When they can keep the margin manageable, their talent can carry them. **Virginia's Kate Galica should dominate this matchup.** Galica set UVA's single-game (17) and single-season (179) draw control records in 2025, was named ACC Midfielder of the Year, and averages 7.4 draws per game in 2026 (22nd nationally, 3rd in ACC). If Galica wins draws at her season average, Virginia will control possession.

## Head coach Jenn Cook brings a defensive mind and rising trajectory

**Jenn Cook** is in her 4th season as head coach (14th year on staff), having succeeded Hall of Famer **Chris Sailer** (36 years, 433 wins) in late 2022. Cook was a **three-time IWLCA All-American and two-time Tewaaraton nominee** at North Carolina, where she holds career records in ground balls (200) and games played (76). She was named **2007 Inside Lacrosse Defender of the Year.**

Her career record as Princeton's head coach is approximately **38-15**, with a clear upward trajectory: an 8-8 transition year in 2023, then 11-7 with an NCAA Tournament appearance in 2024, then **16-4 with an Ivy League championship and NCAA Quarterfinals appearance in 2025**. Her staff won the **2025 Ivy League Coaching Staff of the Year** and **IWLCA Mid-Atlantic Coaching Staff of the Year**.

Cook's tactical identity centers on defensive organization, balanced offensive scoring, and high-volume shooting. She emphasizes consistency and composure, telling the Daily Princetonian: "Our focus so far this season has been on consistency and discipline for a full sixty minutes." Her offensive philosophy prioritizes ball movement and multiple playmakers over isolation — Princeton had three players in the national Top 30 in points per game in 2025.

## How Virginia matches up against Princeton's style

Princeton's offensive identity is **high-volume, balanced, motion-based attack** — they ranked 4th nationally in shots per game in 2025. They are not isolation-heavy; they move the ball through Dora to create open looks for Morrisroe, MacDonald, and their midfield scorers. This is more similar to the ball-movement offense Virginia saw from Notre Dame (a 9-7 UVA win) than the power attack of Maryland or Stanford.

**Virginia's three primary advantages:**

- **Draw controls.** This is the matchup that could decide the game. Galica vs. Princeton's patchwork draw unit is a mismatch Virginia must exploit to create extra possessions.
- **Scoring runs.** Princeton has allowed a 9-0 run (Loyola Q2) and a 7-0 run (Maryland Q1) this season. Virginia's offense, which scored 7 unanswered against Pitt, is capable of delivering these knockout stretches.
- **Early-game pressure.** Princeton has been slow to start in all 4 games. Virginia should come out aggressive and try to build a lead before Princeton's second-half adjustment ability kicks in.

**Princeton's advantages:**

- **Individual offensive talent.** Morrisroe, MacDonald, and Dora are each capable of taking over a game. MacDonald just had an 8-point performance at Harvard. Virginia needs Allen-level individual matchups on at least two of these three.
- **Hughes in goal.** Her 11.25 saves per game will frustrate Virginia shooters. Virginia must be disciplined in shot selection.
- **Second-half adjustments.** Princeton has outscored opponents in the second half in 3 of 4 games. Even if Virginia leads at halftime, the game won't be over.

**Princeton's ranking trajectory:** The Tigers entered 2026 ranked **#5-6** across polls and were voted **#1 in the Ivy League preseason poll** (14 of 16 first-place votes). After the Loyola and Maryland losses, they dropped to **#18-20** in the IWLCA/Nike/USA Lacrosse polls. The Harvard win should stabilize or slightly improve that number when the March 9 poll releases. Yale (6-0, #6 nationally) has overtaken Princeton as the Ivy's top-ranked team.

## Conclusion: exploit the draw circle and attack early

Princeton is a talented team in transition — still elite at the skill positions but searching for identity in the draw circle and trying to find the consistency that made them a 16-win Ivy champion a year ago. **The loss of Whiteway's 249 career draw controls and Blake's 89-goal season has fundamentally changed who Princeton is**, and they're not yet fully adjusted.

Virginia's game plan should center on three priorities. First, **win draws decisively** through Galica; every extra possession magnifies Virginia's improving offensive chemistry. Second, **attack early and attack fast** — Princeton's slow starts and vulnerability to scoring runs mean the first 15 minutes could set the tone for the entire game. Third, **disrupt the Dora-MacDonald-Morrisroe passing triangle** through aggressive defensive positioning in passing lanes; if Dora can't distribute, Princeton's offense becomes far more predictable.

The biggest risk for Virginia is complacency. Princeton has shown it can come roaring back — the 8-3 second-half run at Loyola and the rally from 9-3 down at Maryland demonstrate resilience. Hughes will keep the Tigers in the game with double-digit saves. And MacDonald, riding the high of her 200th career point, is playing her best lacrosse of the season.

This should be a close, competitive game between two ranked programs with deep history. Virginia's draw-control advantage and home-field edge at Klöckner Stadium provide a meaningful margin, but Princeton's individual talent — particularly Morrisroe's scoring ability and Dora's vision — means Virginia cannot afford the defensive lapses that characterized its early-season losses.
